Ok, you've made your first "Hello World" app and covered the basis of RoR, now comes the question: what can RoR do for me other that having a sweet-ass syntax and a mind-blowing framework?

Quick answer: Gems, a renowned and ever growing community at stackoverflow.com and the assurance that the guys behind RoR's code take security and performance issues, very serious.

Almost every web app have something in common with one another and this is where Gems come into play, its boring and demotivating to repeat or re-do code for a certain functionality that someone-else might probably have done it better and more efficiently then you.

So here's a list of some basic needs that you'll probably have and can easily solve by just installing a Gem and readying its configuration/usage documentation:

  • Users and authentication: Devise, RailsCast episode.
  • Integrating your users with Facebook, twitter, etc.: Omniauth, RailsCast episode.
  • Authorization: Declarative Authorization, RailsCast episode.
  • Pagination for large lists: Kaminari, RailsCast episode.
  • State machine, for models that need different behaviors for different states: State Machine.
  • Handling attachments (ready to work with S3): Paperclip, RailsCast episode.
  • Move jobs into the background for asynchronous processing: sidekiq, RailsCast episode.
  • Google Maps: gmaps4rails.

For more on Gems, take a look at The Ruby Toolbox and be sure to keep RailsCast close to you, its by far the best source of RoR material out there.

I generally use these two sites has a point of reference to know if a specific Gem is a trustworthy one, or if there's a better alternative.
